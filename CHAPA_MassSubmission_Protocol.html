PART 1 — TARGET SELECTION STRATEGY

Simultaneous submission to every member of Congress is quantity without precision. The pressure strategy with maximum return focuses on three tiers: members with direct jurisdictional standing, members with documented public positions aligned with this bill, and members representing competitive districts where constituent pressure has disproportionate leverage.

These members sit on the exact committees with referral authority over this bill. A citizen petition submitted to a member who can move it in committee is exponentially more effective than one submitted to a member who cannot.

Tier 3 — Pressure Targets in Competitive Districts (submit Day 3)

These members represent swing districts where 73% voter support for surveillance reform is a liability if they ignore the petition. Submit after Tier 1 and 2 are logged — so the pressure leaderboard has visible entries when these offices receive the submission.

Targeting note: The House Judiciary Subcommittee on Crime and Federal Government Surveillance has direct referral authority over Sections 1–3, 6, 8, 10, and 11. The House Energy & Commerce Subcommittee on Communications has referral authority over Sections 5, 7, 9, and 11(d). Dual referral is strategically advantageous — it creates two committee pressure points and two sets of members who can champion the bill. Address Tier 3 submissions to both subcommittee chairs simultaneously.

Exhibit D · Mass Submission Protocol
30-DAY FOLLOW-UP SEQUENCE
Execute this sequence from submission date · Do not skip steps · The sequence is the pressure
Day 0 — Submission Day
Send fax to all Tier 1 and Tier 2 targets simultaneously (same window, same day)
Drop certified mail packages at post office for all Tier 1 and Tier 2 targets
Submit web contact forms for all Tier 1 and Tier 2 targets
Log each submission in the Submission Log with timestamp and delivery confirmation number
Post on public record: "CHAPA submitted to [X] members of Congress on [date]. Watching the clock."
Day 3 — Tier 3 Submission
Send fax, certified mail, and web form to all Tier 3 targets
Update submission log
Note publicly: "CHAPA now submitted to [X] members. Tier 1 response window: [date]."
Day 10 — First Follow-Up
Call the DC office of each Tier 1 target. Ask to speak with the Legislative Correspondent by name where known. Confirm receipt. Note name of person you spoke with in the log.
Script: "I'm [Name] from [City, NM]. I submitted a citizen petition on [date] — the Citizen's Hardware and Airspace Protection Act. I'm calling to confirm receipt and ask if the LC has had a chance to review it." Nothing more. Record the response verbatim.
Day 20 — Second Follow-Up
Send a one-paragraph follow-up fax to all non-responding Tier 1 and Tier 2 offices. Text: "This is a follow-up to petition CHAPA-2026-[###] submitted on [date]. Ten days remain in the 30-day response window. The petition has now been submitted to [X] congressional offices. I look forward to your response."
Update submission log with follow-up timestamp
Day 30 — Response Deadline
Compile the leaderboard: who responded (and how), who has not
Publish the leaderboard publicly with office names and response status
Send a final one-paragraph fax to all non-responding offices: "As of today, your office has not responded to petition CHAPA-2026-[###]. This is the final notification before a FOIA request is filed for your office's petition log. That request will be filed in 30 days."
Day 60 — FOIA Filing Day
File FOIA requests with each non-responding office using the template below
Publish confirmation of FOIA filings publicly
Contact local news outlets in the district of each non-responding member with the leaderboard and the FOIA confirmation

On the leaderboard: The public petition response tracker does not require a website. A publicly shared Google Sheet, a post on a community forum, or a letter to the editor of your local paper citing "as of [date], the following members have not responded to a formal citizen petition" is sufficient to create public record and local news interest. The goal is not viral — it is documented.
