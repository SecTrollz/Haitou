119th Congress  ·  2nd Session  ·  Citizen Petition Draft
THE CITIZEN'S HARDWARE & AIRSPACE
PROTECTION ACT
A Bill to protect the constitutional rights of United States persons in their electronic communications, devices, and the electromagnetic spectrum; to close loopholes that permit warrantless surveillance by federal agencies and commercial entities; to reform the Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act; and for other purposes.
Constitutional Authority: This Act is enacted pursuant to Article I, Section 4; the First, Fourth, and Ninth Amendments to the Constitution of the United States; and the inherent police power of Congress to regulate interstate commerce under Article I, Section 8, Clause 3. The electromagnetic spectrum is a public resource held in trust for the benefit of the people of the United States.

Section 1
Repeal and Replacement of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act
(a) Full Repeal. The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978 (50 U.S.C. § 1801 et seq.) and the FISA Amendments Act of 2008 (Public Law 110-261), including all provisions thereof and any reauthorizations thereof, are hereby repealed in their entirety, effective 180 days after the date of enactment of this Act.
(b) Universal Warrant Mandate. No department, agency, officer, or employee of the United States shall conduct electronic surveillance targeting, in whole or in part, any United States person for any foreign intelligence purpose unless—
(1) a judicial officer of the United States has issued a warrant upon a finding of probable cause, supported by oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the person to be surveilled, the communications to be intercepted, and the specific foreign intelligence purpose to be served; and
(2) such warrant is individualized to the named United States person and may not be issued in bulk or on the basis of a general population or pattern of activity.
(c) Sunset of Existing Collection. Any ongoing collection authorized under the repealed authorities shall cease upon the effective date of repeal. Data collected under such authorities that has not been the subject of a criminal charge or ongoing court proceeding shall be destroyed within 90 days of the effective date of repeal, with a sworn certification of destruction filed with the relevant district court.
Section 2
Closing the Backdoor Search Loophole
(a) Warrant Required for U.S. Person Queries. Notwithstanding any other provision of law, no officer, employee, or agent of the United States may query any database containing communications collected or retained under any foreign intelligence authority—including but not limited to any authority superseded by Section 1 of this Act—using search terms reasonably likely to return communications of or concerning a United States person, unless such officer, employee, or agent first obtains a warrant issued upon probable cause by a federal court of competent jurisdiction.
(b) Prohibition on Retroactive Laundering. The destruction requirements of Section 1(c) shall not be delayed or avoided by converting a foreign intelligence database into a criminal intelligence database or by transferring such database to any other department or agency of the United States.
(c) Reporting Requirement. The Attorney General shall report to Congress within 60 days of the effective date of this Act, and annually thereafter, the number of queries made of any foreign intelligence database during the preceding 12 months that returned communications of United States persons, and the number for which a warrant was and was not obtained.
Section 3
Closing the Data Broker Loophole
(a) Definition of Protected Communications Data. For purposes of this section, "Protected Communications Data" means any information created, transmitted, inferred, or derived in connection with the use of an electronic device or communications network, including but not limited to—
(1) Location Data: any data sufficient to identify the past, present, or likely future physical location of a device or its user, including GPS coordinates, cell tower association records, Wi-Fi access point association records, and Bluetooth proximity logs;
(2) Association Data: any data sufficient to identify the persons, entities, or services with whom a device user has communicated or associated, including call detail records, message logs, contact lists, and application usage logs;
(3) Behavioral Data: any data sufficient to reveal patterns of activity, belief, political affiliation, religious practice, health status, or intimate life, including browsing history, purchase records, and biometric data.
(b) Prohibition on Federal Purchase Without Warrant. No department or agency of the United States, including but not limited to the Federal Bureau of Investigation, the National Security Agency, the Department of Homeland Security, the Internal Revenue Service, the Drug Enforcement Administration, and the Department of Defense, shall purchase, license, obtain, or otherwise access Protected Communications Data from any commercial data broker, carrier, or third-party aggregator without first obtaining a warrant issued upon probable cause by a federal court of competent jurisdiction.
(c) Prohibition on Data Broker Sales to Federal Government. No commercial data broker, carrier, or third-party aggregator shall sell, license, transfer, or otherwise provide Protected Communications Data to any department or agency of the United States without verifying the existence of a valid judicial warrant authorizing such transfer. Provision of data in reliance on a facially valid warrant shall not itself constitute a violation of this subsection.
Penalty: Any person or entity that knowingly violates subsection (c) shall be liable for a civil penalty of not less than $100,000 per individual affected per transaction, and shall be subject to disgorgement of all consideration received for such transfer. Willful violations shall constitute a felony punishable by imprisonment of not more than 10 years.
Section 4
Reversal of the Third-Party Doctrine as Applied to Electronic Data
(a) Reasonable Expectation of Privacy — Statutory Recognition. Any United States person retains a reasonable expectation of privacy under the Fourth Amendment to the Constitution in all electronic data created, stored, or transmitted on or through—
(1) any device owned, leased, or paid for by that person;
(2) any communications network subscribed to or paid for by that person; or
(3) any cloud service or remote storage associated with that person's account.
(b) No Transfer of Privacy Rights. The transmission of data to a carrier, service provider, or other third party in the ordinary course of using a communications service does not constitute a voluntary relinquishment of the sender's privacy rights in that data. No carrier or service provider shall claim ownership of, or assert a right to disclose without consent, any data transmitted to it in the course of providing a service.
(c) Geofence Warrant Limitation. No federal, state, or local law enforcement agency shall obtain or execute a geofence warrant—defined as any warrant seeking electronic data for all devices within a defined geographic area or time window—that is not specifically tailored to an identified individual or individuals named with particularity. A geofence warrant that encompasses devices not otherwise under individualized suspicion shall be void ab initio.
Section 5
Reform of CALEA and Revocation of Carrier Immunity
(a) Repeal of Compelled Surveillance Expansion. Any provision of the Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act (47 U.S.C. § 1001 et seq.) or implementing regulation that obligates a provider of free or publicly accessible Wi-Fi service—including libraries, educational institutions, houses of worship, coffee shops, public transit providers, and municipal networks—to comply with demands for interception or surveillance absent a valid judicial warrant is hereby repealed.
(b) No Mandatory Encryption Backdoor. Nothing in CALEA, as amended, shall be construed to require any carrier or service provider to—
(1) install or maintain any capability that deliberately weakens or circumvents end-to-end encryption offered to users; or
(2) retain plaintext or decryptable copies of communications that the user has chosen to encrypt.
(c) Revocation of Blanket Immunity. No immunity from civil or criminal liability under any provision of federal law shall apply to any carrier, service provider, or telecommunications equipment manufacturer that—
(1) knowingly facilitates warrantless interception of the communications of a United States person;
(2) knowingly installs or maintains a surveillance capability not authorized by a valid judicial order; or
(3) knowingly sells or transfers customer data in violation of Section 3 of this Act.
Section 6
Citizen Lawsuit Provision
(a) Private Right of Action. Any United States person who suffers a violation of this Act shall have a private right of action in any United States district court against—
(1) any carrier, service provider, data broker, or telecommunications equipment manufacturer that violates any provision of this Act; and
(2) any officer or employee of the United States who, acting under color of law and outside the scope of a valid judicial authorization, violates any provision of this Act.
(b) Remedies. A prevailing plaintiff shall be entitled to—
(1) actual damages, including damages for emotional distress, reputational harm, and loss of associational freedom;
(2) statutory damages of not less than $10,000 and not more than $250,000 per violation;
(3) punitive damages upon a showing of willful or reckless disregard for the rights of the plaintiff; and
(4) reasonable attorneys' fees and costs.
(c) Waiver of Sovereign Immunity. The United States and its agencies hereby waive sovereign immunity for claims arising under this Act. The doctrine of qualified immunity shall not apply to claims brought under this Act against officers or employees of the United States acting in a surveillance capacity.
(d) Statute of Limitations. A cause of action under this section shall accrue upon the date the plaintiff discovered, or with reasonable diligence should have discovered, the violation. No cause of action shall be brought more than five years after the date of discovery.
Section 7
The Airspace Mandate — Electromagnetic Spectrum as Protected Commons
(a) Declaration of Protected Commons. The electromagnetic spectrum allocated for commercial communications use by the Federal Communications Commission is hereby declared a protected commons held in trust for the benefit of the people of the United States. No license, contract, or regulatory approval shall be construed to convey to any carrier, service provider, or private entity any proprietary right to intercept, retain, or analyze the content of transmissions made over that spectrum.
