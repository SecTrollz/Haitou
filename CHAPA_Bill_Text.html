119th Congress  ·  2nd Session  ·  Citizen Petition Draft
THE CITIZEN'S HARDWARE & AIRSPACE
PROTECTION ACT
A Bill to protect the constitutional rights of United States persons in their electronic communications, devices, and the electromagnetic spectrum; to close loopholes that permit warrantless surveillance by federal agencies and commercial entities; to reform the Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act; and for other purposes.
Constitutional Authority: This Act is enacted pursuant to Article I, Section 4; the First, Fourth, and Ninth Amendments to the Constitution of the United States; and the inherent police power of Congress to regulate interstate commerce under Article I, Section 8, Clause 3. The electromagnetic spectrum is a public resource held in trust for the benefit of the people of the United States.

Section 1
Repeal and Replacement of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act
(a) Full Repeal. The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978 (50 U.S.C. § 1801 et seq.) and the FISA Amendments Act of 2008 (Public Law 110-261), including all provisions thereof and any reauthorizations thereof, are hereby repealed in their entirety, effective 180 days after the date of enactment of this Act.
(b) Universal Warrant Mandate. No department, agency, officer, or employee of the United States shall conduct electronic surveillance targeting, in whole or in part, any United States person for any foreign intelligence purpose unless—
(1) a judicial officer of the United States has issued a warrant upon a finding of probable cause, supported by oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the person to be surveilled, the communications to be intercepted, and the specific foreign intelligence purpose to be served; and
(2) such warrant is individualized to the named United States person and may not be issued in bulk or on the basis of a general population or pattern of activity.
(c) Sunset of Existing Collection. Any ongoing collection authorized under the repealed authorities shall cease upon the effective date of repeal. Data collected under such authorities that has not been the subject of a criminal charge or ongoing court proceeding shall be destroyed within 90 days of the effective date of repeal, with a sworn certification of destruction filed with the relevant district court.
Section 2
Closing the Backdoor Search Loophole
(a) Warrant Required for U.S. Person Queries. Notwithstanding any other provision of law, no officer, employee, or agent of the United States may query any database containing communications collected or retained under any foreign intelligence authority—including but not limited to any authority superseded by Section 1 of this Act—using search terms reasonably likely to return communications of or concerning a United States person, unless such officer, employee, or agent first obtains a warrant issued upon probable cause by a federal court of competent jurisdiction.
(b) Prohibition on Retroactive Laundering. The destruction requirements of Section 1(c) shall not be delayed or avoided by converting a foreign intelligence database into a criminal intelligence database or by transferring such database to any other department or agency of the United States.
(c) Reporting Requirement. The Attorney General shall report to Congress within 60 days of the effective date of this Act, and annually thereafter, the number of queries made of any foreign intelligence database during the preceding 12 months that returned communications of United States persons, and the number for which a warrant was and was not obtained.
Section 3
Closing the Data Broker Loophole
(a) Definition of Protected Communications Data. For purposes of this section, "Protected Communications Data" means any information created, transmitted, inferred, or derived in connection with the use of an electronic device or communications network, including but not limited to—
(1) Location Data: any data sufficient to identify the past, present, or likely future physical location of a device or its user, including GPS coordinates, cell tower association records, Wi-Fi access point association records, and Bluetooth proximity logs;
(2) Association Data: any data sufficient to identify the persons, entities, or services with whom a device user has communicated or associated, including call detail records, message logs, contact lists, and application usage logs;
(3) Behavioral Data: any data sufficient to reveal patterns of activity, belief, political affiliation, religious practice, health status, or intimate life, including browsing history, purchase records, and biometric data.
(b) Prohibition on Federal Purchase Without Warrant. No department or agency of the United States, including but not limited to the Federal Bureau of Investigation, the National Security Agency, the Department of Homeland Security, the Internal Revenue Service, the Drug Enforcement Administration, and the Department of Defense, shall purchase, license, obtain, or otherwise access Protected Communications Data from any commercial data broker, carrier, or third-party aggregator without first obtaining a warrant issued upon probable cause by a federal court of competent jurisdiction.
(c) Prohibition on Data Broker Sales to Federal Government. No commercial data broker, carrier, or third-party aggregator shall sell, license, transfer, or otherwise provide Protected Communications Data to any department or agency of the United States without verifying the existence of a valid judicial warrant authorizing such transfer. Provision of data in reliance on a facially valid warrant shall not itself constitute a violation of this subsection.
Penalty: Any person or entity that knowingly violates subsection (c) shall be liable for a civil penalty of not less than $100,000 per individual affected per transaction, and shall be subject to disgorgement of all consideration received for such transfer. Willful violations shall constitute a felony punishable by imprisonment of not more than 10 years.
Section 4
Reversal of the Third-Party Doctrine as Applied to Electronic Data
(a) Reasonable Expectation of Privacy — Statutory Recognition. Any United States person retains a reasonable expectation of privacy under the Fourth Amendment to the Constitution in all electronic data created, stored, or transmitted on or through—
(1) any device owned, leased, or paid for by that person;
(2) any communications network subscribed to or paid for by that person; or
(3) any cloud service or remote storage associated with that person's account.
(b) No Transfer of Privacy Rights. The transmission of data to a carrier, service provider, or other third party in the ordinary course of using a communications service does not constitute a voluntary relinquishment of the sender's privacy rights in that data. No carrier or service provider shall claim ownership of, or assert a right to disclose without consent, any data transmitted to it in the course of providing a service.
(c) Geofence Warrant Limitation. No federal, state, or local law enforcement agency shall obtain or execute a geofence warrant—defined as any warrant seeking electronic data for all devices within a defined geographic area or time window—that is not specifically tailored to an identified individual or individuals named with particularity. A geofence warrant that encompasses devices not otherwise under individualized suspicion shall be void ab initio.
Section 5
Reform of CALEA and Revocation of Carrier Immunity
(a) Repeal of Compelled Surveillance Expansion. Any provision of the Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act (47 U.S.C. § 1001 et seq.) or implementing regulation that obligates a provider of free or publicly accessible Wi-Fi service—including libraries, educational institutions, houses of worship, coffee shops, public transit providers, and municipal networks—to comply with demands for interception or surveillance absent a valid judicial warrant is hereby repealed.
(b) No Mandatory Encryption Backdoor. Nothing in CALEA, as amended, shall be construed to require any carrier or service provider to—
(1) install or maintain any capability that deliberately weakens or circumvents end-to-end encryption offered to users; or
(2) retain plaintext or decryptable copies of communications that the user has chosen to encrypt.
(c) Revocation of Blanket Immunity. No immunity from civil or criminal liability under any provision of federal law shall apply to any carrier, service provider, or telecommunications equipment manufacturer that—
(1) knowingly facilitates warrantless interception of the communications of a United States person;
(2) knowingly installs or maintains a surveillance capability not authorized by a valid judicial order; or
(3) knowingly sells or transfers customer data in violation of Section 3 of this Act.
Section 6
Citizen Lawsuit Provision
(a) Private Right of Action. Any United States person who suffers a violation of this Act shall have a private right of action in any United States district court against—
(1) any carrier, service provider, data broker, or telecommunications equipment manufacturer that violates any provision of this Act; and
(2) any officer or employee of the United States who, acting under color of law and outside the scope of a valid judicial authorization, violates any provision of this Act.
(b) Remedies. A prevailing plaintiff shall be entitled to—
(1) actual damages, including damages for emotional distress, reputational harm, and loss of associational freedom;
(2) statutory damages of not less than $10,000 and not more than $250,000 per violation;
(3) punitive damages upon a showing of willful or reckless disregard for the rights of the plaintiff; and
(4) reasonable attorneys' fees and costs.
(c) Waiver of Sovereign Immunity. The United States and its agencies hereby waive sovereign immunity for claims arising under this Act. The doctrine of qualified immunity shall not apply to claims brought under this Act against officers or employees of the United States acting in a surveillance capacity.
(d) Statute of Limitations. A cause of action under this section shall accrue upon the date the plaintiff discovered, or with reasonable diligence should have discovered, the violation. No cause of action shall be brought more than five years after the date of discovery.
Section 7
The Airspace Mandate — Electromagnetic Spectrum as Protected Commons
(a) Declaration of Protected Commons. The electromagnetic spectrum allocated for commercial communications use by the Federal Communications Commission is hereby declared a protected commons held in trust for the benefit of the people of the United States. No license, contract, or regulatory approval shall be construed to convey to any carrier, service provider, or private entity any proprietary right to intercept, retain, or analyze the content of transmissions made over that spectrum.
(b) Presumption of Privacy. Any transmission made over the electromagnetic spectrum by or on behalf of a United States person is presumptively private. This presumption may be rebutted only by a valid judicial warrant issued upon probable cause.
(c) Carrier Transmission Encryption Requirement. Within three years of the enactment of this Act, all carriers providing mobile voice and data services shall implement end-to-end or transport-layer encryption sufficient to prevent interception of communications by any party other than the sender and intended recipient during transmission over any segment of their network, including satellite downlink segments. The FCC shall promulgate regulations establishing minimum encryption standards within one year of enactment.
(d) Third-Party Interception Prohibition. No person or entity, including a carrier, may intercept, store, or perform computational analysis upon any transmission made over the protected electromagnetic commons without—
(1) the express, revocable, and granular written consent of the transmitting party; or
(2) a valid judicial warrant.
Section 8 Closes: Stingray Pen Register Loophole
Cell-Site Simulator Warrant Requirement
(a) Finding. Congress finds that cell-site simulator devices ("CSS devices"), commonly known as Stingrays, IMSI catchers, or DirtBoxes, compel all nearby mobile devices—regardless of whether any such device is the lawful target of investigation—to identify themselves and reveal their precise physical location. The operation of a CSS device constitutes a search under the Fourth Amendment and may not be authorized by a pen register order or any process issued on a standard below probable cause.
(b) Warrant Required. No federal, state, local, or tribal law enforcement agency shall deploy or operate a CSS device without first obtaining a warrant issued by a federal or state court of competent jurisdiction upon a finding of probable cause, particularly describing—
(1) the specific device or devices to be located;
(2) the geographic area within which the device will be operated;
(3) the maximum duration of operation; and
(4) the procedures to be used to immediately purge all data collected from devices other than the target device.
(c) Mandatory Purge Requirement. All data collected by a CSS device from non-target devices shall be purged automatically and in real time to the extent technologically feasible, and in no event shall such data be retained for more than 24 hours after collection. No data from a non-target device may be queried, transferred, shared, or used for any purpose whatsoever.
(d) Prohibition on Pen Register Authorization. The Pen Register Act (18 U.S.C. §§ 3121–3127) shall not be construed to authorize the operation of any CSS device, and no court may issue a pen register order as the sole authorization for CSS device deployment.
Section 9 Closes: Tower Under 60 Feet · Metadata Retention Mandate
Communications Infrastructure Transparency and Data Minimization
(a) Universal Small Cell Registration. Any communications infrastructure device—including small cell antennas, distributed antenna system nodes, and any structure supporting radio frequency transmission—installed at a height exceeding ten (10) feet above grade on any public or private property within the United States shall be registered with the Federal Communications Commission within 30 days of installation. Such registration shall include—
(1) the GPS coordinates of the installation to an accuracy of within 10 meters;
(2) the identity of the owner and operator;
(3) the frequency bands and power levels at which the device operates;
(4) the data capabilities of the device, including whether the device is capable of storing, logging, or transmitting device identifier information from nearby mobile devices; and
(5) the identity of any entity with access to data collected by or through the device.
(b) Public Registry. The FCC shall maintain a publicly accessible, searchable, machine-readable registry of all registered communications infrastructure devices. The registry shall be updated within 72 hours of receipt of any registration or modification filing.
Penalty for Non-Registration: Any carrier or operator that fails to register a covered device within the required period shall be liable for a civil penalty of not less than $50,000 per day per unregistered device. Knowing concealment of a registrable device shall constitute a felony punishable by imprisonment of not more than five years.
(c) Metadata Retention Reform. The following requirements apply to all carriers subject to FCC jurisdiction:
(1) Retention Limit. No carrier shall retain call detail records, message metadata, or device connection logs relating to a United States person for more than 90 days after the date of creation, except as required by a valid judicial order specifically requiring retention for a named investigation.
(2) Warrant Required for Access. No federal, state, or local law enforcement agency shall obtain call detail records or message metadata from a carrier without a warrant issued upon probable cause. A subpoena, administrative order, or pen register order shall not constitute sufficient process for obtaining such records.
(3) Billing Data Exception. Nothing in this subsection prohibits a carrier from retaining billing records to the extent necessary to resolve a bona fide billing dispute, provided that such records are accessible only to carrier billing personnel and may not be accessed by law enforcement without a warrant.
Section 10 Closes: NSL Gag Orders · Predictive Policing Algorithms
National Security Letter Reform and Prohibition on Predictive Profiling
(a) Judicial Authorization Required for NSLs. The National Security Letter provisions of the Right to Financial Privacy Act (12 U.S.C. § 3414), the Electronic Communications Privacy Act (18 U.S.C. § 2709), and the Fair Credit Reporting Act (15 U.S.C. § 1681u) are amended to require that any National Security Letter ("NSL") be—
(1) authorized by a federal district court judge upon a finding that the information sought is relevant to an authorized investigation and that specific articulable facts support the relevance finding; and
(2) accompanied by a judicial determination that any non-disclosure requirement is necessary to prevent serious harm to the investigation, with such non-disclosure requirement subject to automatic expiration after 180 days unless renewed by judicial order.
(b) Right of Notice. Within 30 days of the expiration or non-renewal of a non-disclosure requirement, the issuing agency shall notify the subject of an NSL that records concerning them were sought. The agency shall disclose the date of issuance, the category of records sought, and whether the records were produced.
(c) Prohibition on Predictive Profiling Programs.
(1) No federal agency, and no state or local agency receiving federal funds under any program, shall operate, fund, or contract for any program or system that—
(A) uses data obtained from a carrier, data broker, or electronic communications service to generate a risk score, threat assessment, or suspicion indicator for any individual who has not been identified as a target of an individualized criminal investigation; or
(B) uses such risk scores, threat assessments, or suspicion indicators to initiate law enforcement contact, obtain a search warrant, or deprive any person of liberty or property.
(2) The prohibition in paragraph (1) applies regardless of whether the underlying data was lawfully obtained and regardless of whether the program is characterized as research, pilot, or operational.
Section 11 Closes: Border Device Search · Device Fingerprinting · Private Spyware
Border Device Protection and Network Security Mandate
(a) Warrant Requirement for Border Device Searches. No officer or employee of U.S. Customs and Border Protection, Immigration and Customs Enforcement, or any other department or agency of the United States shall conduct a forensic examination of any electronic device at a port of entry without—
(1) a judicial warrant issued upon probable cause; or
(2) the express, voluntary, and informed written consent of the device owner.
(b) Definition of Forensic Examination. For purposes of subsection (a), "forensic examination" means any search of the contents of an electronic device beyond a manual review of files or applications currently visible on the unlocked screen, including any extraction, copying, or remote transmission of device data.
(c) Basic Search Permitted. A non-forensic manual review of an electronic device at the border may be conducted upon reasonable suspicion articulated in writing by the reviewing officer. All data collected from such a review that does not result in a criminal referral shall be destroyed within 72 hours.
(d) Carrier Duty to Detect and Block Commercial Spyware.
(1) Duty of Network Security. Every carrier providing mobile communications service in the United States shall implement and maintain network-level detection and blocking capabilities reasonably designed to prevent the installation, operation, or data exfiltration of commercial surveillance software—including zero-click exploit payloads—on subscriber devices connected to its network.
(2) Notification Duty. Upon detecting traffic consistent with the installation or operation of commercial spyware on a subscriber's device, a carrier shall notify the subscriber within 24 hours of detection. Such notification shall not be suppressed by any non-disclosure requirement absent a specific judicial order.
(3) MMS and SS7 Security. Carriers shall implement filtering and validation protocols for Multimedia Messaging Service (MMS) and Signaling System No. 7 (SS7) traffic sufficient to prevent the use of such protocols to extract device identification information or deliver exploit payloads without subscriber interaction. The FCC shall promulgate minimum technical standards within 18 months of enactment.
(e) Prohibition on Provision of Surveillance Tools to Foreign Governments. No United States carrier shall provide network access, subscriber data, or technical assistance to any foreign government or private entity for the purpose of deploying commercial spyware against any person within the territorial United States.
Section 12
Definitions

For purposes of this Act:

"Carrier" means any provider of electronic communications services, including mobile voice, mobile data, fixed broadband, satellite communications, and voice over internet protocol services, that offers services to the public or a class of the public.
"Commercial spyware" means any software or exploit payload designed to be installed on a device without the knowledge or consent of the device owner, for the purpose of exfiltrating data, monitoring communications, or enabling remote access, marketed or licensed by a private commercial entity to governmental or non-governmental customers.
"Electronic device" means any device capable of creating, storing, or transmitting electronic data, including smartphones, tablets, laptops, desktop computers, wearable devices, and vehicle telematics systems.
"Federal agency" means any executive department, military department, government corporation, government-controlled corporation, or independent regulatory agency of the United States, as defined in 5 U.S.C. § 101 et seq.
"United States person" means any United States citizen, lawful permanent resident, or juridical entity organized under the laws of the United States or any state thereof.
"Warrant" means a judicial order issued by a federal or state court of competent jurisdiction upon a finding of probable cause, supported by sworn affidavit, particularly describing the person, place, or thing to be searched or seized, as required by the Fourth Amendment to the Constitution of the United States.
Section 13
Severability

If any provision of this Act, or the application of any provision to any person or circumstance, is held invalid by a court of competent jurisdiction, the remainder of this Act and the application of its provisions to any other person or circumstance shall not be affected thereby.

Section 14
Effective Date

Except as otherwise specifically provided, this Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of its enactment. The repeal of FISA under Section 1 shall take effect on the same date. Regulations required by this Act shall be promulgated on the schedule specified in each section; where no schedule is specified, the relevant agency shall promulgate final regulations within one year of enactment.
