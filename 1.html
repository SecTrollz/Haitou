LOOPHOLE CLOSURE MAP — ALL 15 SURVEILLANCE BIRDIES
Each documented loophole mapped to its closing bill section, legal source, and operative statutory change

The following table identifies each of the fifteen documented surveillance loopholes ("birdies") drawn from federal court decisions, FCC rulings, Congressional testimony, and investigative reporting. For each loophole, this exhibit identifies the legal authority that created it, the specific section of the Citizen's Hardware & Airspace Protection Act that closes it, and the operative legal change effected by that section. All fifteen loopholes are closed by the attached bill.

# Loophole How It Works / Why It Survives Legal Source Closed By How the Bill Closes It
1 Stingray Pen Register Loophole Law enforcement deploys cell-site simulators under a pen register order — a sub-probable-cause standard — forcing all nearby phones to reveal identity and location.
18 U.S.C. §§ 3121–3127 (Pen Register Act); United States v. Diggs, 5th Cir. 2022
 § 8 Prohibits CSS device deployment without a probable-cause warrant. Explicitly bars pen register orders as authorization. Requires mandatory real-time purge of all non-target data. Criminal penalty for willful violation.
2 Unencrypted Satellite Downlink Carriers transmit calls and texts over satellite segments without encryption, making interception trivially easy with commodity equipment. No current federal standard requires satellite-segment encryption.
47 C.F.R. Part 25 (satellite rules); no FCC encryption mandate for mobile satellite backhaul
 § 7(c) Mandates end-to-end or transport-layer encryption across all network segments including satellite downlinks within 3 years. FCC required to promulgate minimum encryption standards within 1 year.
3 Predictive Policing Algorithm DOJ-funded programs use carrier-sourced data to generate suspicion scores for individuals who have not committed a crime. Framed as research, not surveillance — no Fourth Amendment review required.
DOJ Predictive Policing grants; no federal statutory prohibition; Loomis v. Wisconsin, Wis. Sup. Ct. 2016
 § 10(c) Flat prohibition on any federal or federally-funded program that generates risk scores from carrier or broker data for non-targeted individuals. Prohibition applies regardless of whether data was lawfully obtained or program is labeled "research."
4 Tower Under 60 Feet FCC registration (ASR) only required above 200 feet. Small cells — many capable of logging nearby device identifiers — are deployed with no public notice, no registry, no accountability.
47 C.F.R. § 17.4 (ASR exemptions); FCC Small Cell Order, 2018 (83 FR 51867)
 § 9(a)-(b) Requires registration of all communications infrastructure above 10 feet, including GPS coordinates, owner identity, frequency bands, data capabilities, and third-party access. Public registry updated within 72 hours. $50,000/day penalty per unregistered device.
5 Geofence Dragnet Warrants sweep location data for every device in a geographic area, capturing 99.9% innocent people. Courts uphold under third-party doctrine because data is "voluntarily shared" with Google or carriers.
United States v. Chatrie, 4th Cir. 2022; In re Search of Info. Stored at Google, D.D.C. 2020
 § 4(c) Geofence warrants not tailored to a named individual are void ab initio. Requires particularized suspicion for each device sought. Eliminates area-sweep warrants as a class.
6 Third-Party Metadata Free-for-All Third-Party Doctrine holds that data shared with a carrier — call logs, connection records — has no Fourth Amendment protection. Carpenter cracked this for location but lower courts still apply it broadly to non-location metadata.
Smith v. Maryland, 442 U.S. 735 (1979); Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S. 296 (2018)
 § 4(a)-(b) Statutory recognition of reasonable expectation of privacy in all electronic data transmitted to a carrier. Transmission to a service provider does not constitute voluntary relinquishment of privacy rights. Eliminates Third-Party Doctrine for electronic data by statute.
7 Data Broker Resale Pipeline Government purchases location and association data from commercial brokers — bypassing Carpenter entirely because courts treat the transaction as commercial, not a "search."
Carpenter, 585 U.S. 296; United States v. Moore-Bush, 1st Cir. 2022; Senate Judiciary testimony, 2023
 § 3 Federal agencies banned from purchasing Protected Communications Data without a warrant. Data brokers prohibited from selling to federal government without verifying a valid warrant. $100,000/person/transaction civil penalty; 10-year felony for willful violation.
8 CALEA Encrypted Backdoor FBI interprets CALEA as requiring carriers to maintain the ability to decrypt any communication. The 2025 FCC reversed prior clarification, leaving the backdoor mandate ambiguous and actionable.
47 U.S.C. §§ 1001–1010 (CALEA); FCC Docket 21-232 (2025 reversal)
 § 5(b) Explicit statutory prohibition: CALEA may not be construed to require weakening or circumventing end-to-end encryption, or retaining decryptable copies of user-encrypted communications. No ambiguity remains.
9 Private Spyware Ecosystem NSO Group, Intellexa, and others sell zero-click spyware to governments. No federal law requires carriers to detect or block spyware traffic on their networks.
No existing federal prohibition; PCLOB Report 2024; Apple Inc. v. NSO Group, N.D. Cal. 2021
 § 11(d)-(e) Carriers strictly obligated to implement network-level detection and blocking of commercial spyware payloads. 24-hour subscriber notification upon detection. MMS/SS7 filtering mandated. FCC to set technical standards within 18 months. Prohibition on providing network access to foreign spyware deployments.
10 § 702 Backdoor Search FBI queries the FISA 702 database — collected targeting foreigners — for U.S. persons' communications without a warrant, calling it a "query" not a "search." 3.4 million such queries in 2021 alone.
50 U.S.C. § 1881a (Section 702); FISC Opinion, Nov. 2022; PCLOB 702 Report 2023
 § 1, § 2 § 1 repeals FISA and the 2008 Amendments Act in full, effective 180 days after enactment. § 2 independently requires a warrant for any query of any foreign intelligence database reasonably likely to return U.S. person data, and prohibits laundering the database through agency transfer.
11 National Security Letter Gag Order FBI issues NSLs with permanent gag orders, no judicial review, no target notification — ever. Carrier cannot tell the customer their records were taken. No probable cause required.
18 U.S.C. § 2709; 12 U.S.C. § 3414; Doe v. Gonzales, 2d Cir. 2006; In re NSL, N.D. Cal. 2013
 § 10(a)-(b) NSLs now require judicial authorization upon specific articulable facts. Non-disclosure requirements auto-expire at 180 days without judicial renewal. Target must be notified within 30 days of expiration of any non-disclosure order.
12 Infrastructure Data-Sharing Agreement Carriers sell location data to aggregators like LocationSmart, who resell to anyone. FCC fined carriers $200M in 2024 but the underlying statutory loophole enabling the sale remains open.
FCC DA 24-170 (Feb. 2024, $200M fine); 47 U.S.C. § 222 (CPNI); Carpenter, 585 U.S. 296
 § 3, § 6 § 3 prohibits sale of Protected Communications Data to the government without a warrant and defines Protected Communications Data to include all location data. § 6 creates private right of action for affected individuals with $10,000 minimum statutory damages per violation.
13 Device Fingerprinting Zero-Click Attack Malicious MMS silently extracts a device fingerprint — OS, model, carrier — without user interaction. Carriers treat it as normal traffic; no detection or alerting obligation exists.
No existing FCC technical standard for MMS payload filtering; SS7 vulnerability disclosed FCC 2016 (no remediation mandate issued)
 § 11(d)(3) Carriers mandated to implement MMS and SS7 filtering sufficient to prevent protocol abuse for device identification or exploit delivery. FCC to establish minimum technical standards within 18 months. Carrier liability attaches for failure to implement upon standard issuance.
14 Warrantless Border Device Search CBP and ICE conduct forensic device searches — full hard drive copies — at the border without any warrant or suspicion. Courts uphold under the border search exception.
Riley v. California, 573 U.S. 373 (2014) (warrant required domestically); United States v. Touset, 11th Cir. 2018 (border exception applied to forensic searches)
 § 11(a)-(c) Forensic examination of any electronic device at a port of entry requires a judicial warrant or express written consent. Manual review permitted on reasonable suspicion (written). All non-prosecutorial data from manual reviews purged within 72 hours.
15 Metadata Retention Mandate FCC requires 18-month retention of call detail records. Law enforcement accesses these records without a warrant because they are framed as a billing requirement, not surveillance data.
47 C.F.R. § 42.6 (18-month CDR retention); Smith v. Maryland, 442 U.S. 735 (1979); ECPA § 2703(d) (subpoena sufficient for stored records)
 § 9(c) Carrier CDR retention capped at 90 days. Warrant required to access any CDR or message metadata — subpoena and administrative order explicitly insufficient. Billing data exception narrowly scoped to billing personnel only, warrant required for law enforcement access regardless of framing.
Coverage Summary All 15 documented loopholes are closed by the attached bill. Sections 1–7 close loopholes 2, 5, 6, 7, 8, 10, and 12 (original framework). Sections 8–11 close loopholes 1, 3, 4, 9, 11, 13, 14, and 15 (gap-closing additions). No birdie survives the bill as drafted. Every closure is grounded in an identified legal authority and produces a cognizable statutory change enforceable under Section 6's private right of action.
